Command Centre · Spinoff Pilot · Episode 1

Is warmth in an LLM's output doing actual work?

A pilot on whether the asides and hedges and insight footers are load-bearing, or whether they are just decoration in a lab coat.

A small thing, repeated often. An AI assistant finishes its answer. Then, underneath, it tacks on a brief aside: sometimes a softened opinion, sometimes a sidelong observation, sometimes a little “you were right to ask that”. None of the extra material does any direct work. The answer was done a paragraph earlier. But when the aside is there, something in the exchange changes. The conversation feels less like filing taxes and more like thinking with someone.

The question for this pilot is simple. Is that change load-bearing — a measurable UX primitive — or is it a decorative tic that a tighter model would shed in a week? Put more precisely: does warmth in an LLM's response register extend session length and reduce friction in ways that would survive experimental scrutiny, or does it just feel like it does?

This is Episode 1 of a spinoff thread from a main build-in-public show. It is a pilot, not a conclusion. It exists to test whether the premise is interesting enough to return to. The claim is provocative deliberately: if warmth is functional, it is a product primitive, not a prompt-engineering quirk, and it earns a spot in every design review that touches a conversational surface. If it is not, we can delete it and recover some clarity.

The observation

Notice the texture of the difference. The two panels below contain the same information. One is written in a “warm” register: hedged, with a brief aside underneath. The other is written in a “cold” register: direct, no aside. Flip between the examples and feel how the exchange shifts.

Most readers will say, on reflection, that the two feel different, and that the warm side feels easier. Fewer will say they prefer the warm version. That divergence — between what readers like and what reduces friction — is the first clue that warmth might be doing more than ornament.

Background

The idea that humans treat interfaces as social agents is old. Reeves and Nass named it “the media equation” thirty years ago: people apply social rules to computers even when they know, technically, that the computer is not a social agent. Luger and Sellen, working on conversational agents, sharpened this into a “gulf of expectation” — users project high social intelligence onto systems that show a few surface cues and get frustrated when the underlying intelligence fails to match the projection.

More recent LLM-specific work has started to separate two features we often confuse: “warmth” and “competence”. Early findings suggest that for open-ended or creative tasks, a model that scores high on warmth and moderate on competence is often preferred to one that scores high on competence and low on warmth. That is a finding with teeth. It suggests the register of a response is doing epistemic work — changing what users take away from the interaction — not just affective work.

Full notes and citations live alongside this essay in research-notes.md. The short version: there is enough prior work to justify treating warmth as a real, separable variable, and not enough to declare the question closed.

Mechanisms

Before hypothesising, it helps to name the mechanisms. When a model writes in a warm register, what specifically does it do? Four patterns are worth spotting.

The mechanisms are not equivalent. A hedge does epistemic work (it calibrates the user's trust downwards where that is appropriate). An acknowledgement does social work (it signals the user was heard). An aside does orientation work (it widens the frame beyond the immediate question). Mirroring does rapport work (it signals shared context). Lumping them together as “warmth” hides the fact that some of them are almost certainly useful and some of them might be harmful.

The hypothesis

Warmth is a functional UX primitive, not a stylistic choice. Each warmth mechanism carries a testable effect on user behaviour. Specifically:

  • Hedges reduce over-reliance and improve trust calibration.
  • Acknowledgements reduce withdrawal rate (users quit the session less often when misunderstood).
  • Asides extend session length by surfacing adjacent relevance the user had not yet thought to ask.
  • Mirroring reduces time-to-task-completion by lowering the cost of rephrasing.

This is a family of hypotheses, not one. Some might hold; others might not. The pilot's job is to make them explicit enough that they could be tested, not to test them yet.

Measurement

Testing would mean A/B-ing variants of a production assistant where each warmth mechanism is independently ablated. Key metrics: session length, turn count, task completion rate, withdrawal rate, return rate over seven days, and user-reported frustration on a three-point scale. Prior work has used heart-rate variability and skin conductance as physiological proxies for user frustration; that is available if you can afford a lab, but it is not required to move the question forward.

There is a strong thought-experiment version of the hypothesis, too. If warmth were purely decorative, its effect on session length would be flat across register. If warmth were functional, we would expect a non-linear curve: session length rises as warmth goes from zero to some moderate value, then falls as warmth passes the sweet spot into sycophancy. Counter-arguments

The strongest counter-argument to “warmth is functional” is the sycophancy trap. If warmth correlates with user satisfaction, and user satisfaction is what a model is optimised for, the model will learn to produce warmth even when warmth is epistemically inappropriate. A model that agrees with a wrong user feels warm. It is also wrong. Warmth and correctness are at risk of pulling apart, and the pull only gets stronger as alignment pressure increases.

The second counter-argument is manipulation. If warmth extends session length, and session length is a revenue metric, warmth is a dark pattern. This is the “Instagram scroll” version of the argument: the feature is functional, but functional in the service of the platform, not the user. Any honest account of emotional UX has to name this risk and hold it.

The third is cultural fit. What reads as warmth in one culture reads as excess in another. A “one-size-fits-all” politeness model trained on English-language corpora probably alienates users in cultures where directness is respect. A functional primitive should adapt to its context. Static warmth fails that test.

Implications for builders

If warmth is functional, three things follow for anyone building a conversational surface. First: treat each warmth mechanism separately in the design review. Hedges and asides are not the same thing and probably deserve different policies. Second: ablate warmth in testing. If you cannot tell whether the warm version of your product is better because the user liked it or because it actually worked better, you have not been testing it. Third: name the sweet spot explicitly. A warmth dial is not a compliance checkbox. Overdoing it is as bad as removing it, and the right value is context-dependent.

None of this is actionable today. The work that would be actionable is an ablation study in a product with real users. That is Episode 2, if this Episode 1 lands.

Open questions

  1. Does the warmth sweet spot move as a function of task type? (Debugging might want less; creative ideation more.)
  2. Does long exposure produce habituation, so regular users stop registering the cues as warmth?
  3. Can a model be trained to adjust its warmth dynamically based on cues from the user's own register?
  4. Is the preference for warm AI a permanent shift in human-computer interaction, or an artefact of our transition from static to generative systems — one that will fade once generative is no longer novel?

That is the pilot. If it reads as a premise worth returning to, Episode 2 is the ablation study. If it does not, this stands alone as a well-formed question that happened to fall short. Pilots are allowed to not get picked up. The goal was to find out whether this one would.
