# Rules versus model — the v0 shape most teams get backwards

*Why the production literature converges on hand-tuned rules for the v0 router, why most teams reach for the classifier first anyway, and why the under-discussed primitive is the taxonomy.*

## The instinct

Ask a competent engineer how to build a routing layer for a multi-architecture agent system and the first instinct is the wrong one. The instinct is *train a classifier*. There is a very clean shape for this answer: take prompts, label them with the architecture that performed best, train a small encoder on the labelled pairs, deploy. Cite RouteLLM. Cite RouterBench. Quote a cost-savings number with a confidence interval. It is the answer that pattern-matches against the academic literature, against the conference papers, against the framework demos, and against the way every previous classification problem in machine learning has been solved. It is also, repeatedly and systematically, the wrong answer for a v0.

The production literature pushes back, hard. Five sources in the Episode 2 cold round converged on a shape that is not the shape the instinct produces: the v0 buildable today is **rules plus instrumentation**, not a model. 6–10 hand-tuned scoring rules over 5–7 features inferable from the task statement, a frontier-LLM fallback for ambiguous cases, and trace fidelity end-to-end. The classifier is the wrong v0. The premature optimisation is the model. The under-discussed primitive — the one that the literature circles around but rarely names — is the **taxonomy**: the human design work that says which task wants which configuration.

## Why the instinct is wrong

The instinct is wrong for a structural reason, not a tactical one. A classifier is a function from features to a label, trained on examples. Three things have to be true for that function to outperform a hand-tuned rule. First, the feature space has to be rich enough that no human can reason about the boundaries by hand. Second, the training data has to be available, in volume, with low label noise. Third, the cost of making a wrong decision has to be observable in feedback — the gradient has to land somewhere. For task-architecture routing, none of those three is reliably true at v0.

The feature space is small. The Episode 2 cold round's convergent feature list ran to five: parallelisability, active tool count, expected context noise, verifier availability, reversibility. Two more get pulled in from ChatGPT's eight-pack — observation deficit and coordination tax. Seven features, all hand-engineerable, all inspectable. A junior engineer can reason about the boundary between *route to chain* and *route to monolith* on a whiteboard in fifteen minutes. There is no exotic interaction term hiding in the data that only a learned model could find. The instinct that a model is necessary because the problem is too high-dimensional is wrong; the problem is not high-dimensional.

The training data is not there. RouteLLM's published 65k-sample preference dataset (`D_arena`) was never actually released; the community fork (Liqs-v2) preprocessed the available crumbs and reports **only 19K usable instances**. The router suffers majority-class overfitting (macro F1 0.23–0.35) because the training data was skewed 51% in favour of the strong model. Reproduction is infeasible. Production execution logs are where the real signal lives, and a solo founder does not have them — Anthropic, OpenAI, Cursor, Cognition, Replit, Sourcegraph all hold meaningful traces, none of which are external. Synthetic generation is the cheap viable path, but its failure modes — *model collapse, fidelity gaps, artifactual relationships* — are well-documented and the trap is circularity: you train a classifier to predict what a strong model thinks, which is just distillation of the strong model's intuitions about routing, which are unknown to be good.

Feedback is delayed and noisy. The cost of a wrong routing decision shows up two or three steps downstream — a hallucinated requirement, a failed test, a token bill three times what was expected, a user retry. By the time the signal arrives, multiple decisions have compounded. Even if the classifier had clean training data, the gradient that tells it whether it was right would arrive too late to matter for online learning, and the offline evaluation it would replace doesn't work either: static *golden datasets* rot as user behaviour evolves, evaluation leakage hides edge cases, deterministic leakage (the model produces conversational text where structured tool calls are required) is a named production failure surface, and Galileo.ai's specific finding is that numeric scoring scales (0–100) introduce more noise than binary (pass/fail) verdicts. The measurement loop the classifier needs to improve doesn't exist yet.

## Why the rules win

Hand-tuned rules win for the inverse reason that the classifier loses. The feature space is small enough to reason about. The boundaries are inspectable. The failure modes are named, not learned. When a rule fires wrongly, the engineer can read the rule and see why. When it fires rightly, the engineer can read the rule and trust it. Trace fidelity is free; the trace *is* the rule. The router never *tries hard* because there is no learning loop to optimise. When the rule disagrees with itself — when the score lands ambiguously — the fallback fires, the frontier-LLM gets called, the answer goes through, and the case gets logged. Six months of those logs become the training data for a v1 classifier that is not running blind.

Cursor's load-bearing line in the original synthesis states the constraint plainly: *"You can't benchmark your way out of the data problem."* The classifier the instinct reaches for is benchmarking its way out of a problem that is fundamentally about labels, not models. The rules the production literature recommends are *generating* the labels, by design, slowly, with full traceability. The rules are the data factory the classifier eventually trains on. Skipping the rule stage is not building a faster router — it is building a router on a foundation that does not exist.

There is a second reason, which the addendum sharpened. The *"When Routing Collapses"* paper (arXiv 2602.03478v1) documents the failure mode that makes a learned router actively dangerous in the wrong production configuration: routing collapse, where the router systematically defaults to the strongest model as cost budgets rise, completely undermining the cost-savings promise. The mechanism is **objective-decision mismatch**: predicting a scalar score versus making an argmax decision. Small prediction errors flip relative rankings. The naive *low-confidence → cheaper architecture* fallback that Episode 2 originally recommended makes this worse, by ratcheting traffic toward the strong model. Interactive 2 ships the inverse rule — low confidence falls back to **monolith**, never to the more expensive route — on the basis of that finding.

A rule-based router cannot collapse. It does not have a learning loop to drift. The configuration is what the engineer wrote. If the wrong configuration is shipping, the engineer rewrites the rule. If the right configuration is shipping but quality is degrading, the cause is upstream — the base model, the prompt, the tool descriptions, the context. The router is the one component the engineer can trust to behave the way it behaved last week. That property is worth more, in the first six months of production, than any learned-model performance figure on a synthetic benchmark.

## The taxonomy is the work

The hardest part of the v0 router is not the rules. It is the **feature taxonomy** — the human design work that names what to measure, what to ignore, and what the boundaries between configurations are. Episode 1's load-bearing line came from Grok's pass through 2026 X discourse: *"the correct position changes per task — but building the router that reliably knows is the real unsolved primitive."* Episode 2 took that line and answered it: the router is buildable, but the part that pays off first is the rules; the part that took the work is the taxonomy.

The taxonomy is where most of the field still hand-waves. Frameworks expose runtime knobs — LangGraph's `Command(goto=..., update=...)`, OpenAI's `handoff(input_filter=...)`, Claude Agent SDK's subagent dispatch, Pydantic AI's programmatic hand-off — but none of them prescribe *which* task should fire *which* knob. The published taxonomies are partial: NVIDIA's Prompt Task and Complexity Classifier survives production contact only when paired with model-criteria frameworks; MasRouter's three-layer cascade is a research demo without a production deployment trail; TRouter / Arch-Router / MoMA exist mostly as papers without GitHub repos; RouteLLM's BERT-class router is the most-cited production example and it suffers majority-class overfitting on a dataset that was never released.

What this means for a solo founder is that the v0 work is taxonomy work. Sit down with the product's task distribution. Name the tasks that come in. Name the configurations that should serve them. Write the rules that bridge the two. Ship the rules. Watch the trace. The interactive in the campaign's `pilot.html` — Router-in-a-box — is the literal shape of that v0: six features, one rule, one fallback, no learned model. The argument the interactive makes is the argument the production literature makes: a router that is honest about being rules and instrumentation will outperform a router that pretends to be a classifier, because the failure modes you can see are the failure modes you can fix.

## The threshold question

The Perplexity recovery in the addendum sharpened one specific question the original synthesis hand-waved at: when should a solo founder build a router at all? Episode 2 named *"narrow task domain"* as the contraindication; Perplexity names the threshold: *"Below ~100–300 high-quality LLM queries per day, the overhead of maintaining a router usually outweighs the cost savings."*

That number is doing real work. Most pre-launch products are below it. Most v1 products are below it. By the time a product is sustainably above it, the founder has accumulated production logs, has a sense of the task distribution that *actually arrives* (versus the one the founder imagined would arrive), and has an empirical feel for which mismatches are costing real money. *That* is the moment to build a router. Before then, the rules in the founder's head — *if it's a code task, use the coding tool; if it's a research task, use the research tool* — are functionally equivalent to a one-line script, and the time saved by writing the script is better spent on the next 100 paying users.

Episode 3's candidate question is *should BorAI build a v0 router right now?* The addendum's commitments paragraph commits to **queued, not now**. BorAI's traffic sits below the 100–300/day threshold. The decision is not the research question — it is the engineering call. No ADR opens until Episode 3's synthesis commits. The shape of that ADR, when it arrives, will be a feature taxonomy, a 100-task validation set, and a fallback policy that doesn't ratchet toward the strong model. Same shape as Interactive 2 in the demo, applied to the actual task distribution of an actual product.

## The closing call

The v0 router most teams reach for is the wrong v0 router. The instinct toward a classifier is structurally correct for problems with rich feature spaces, abundant clean labels, and observable feedback gradients — and routing has none of those. The production literature converges on rules. The contrarian discourse, what little of it the addendum could surface, agrees. The under-discussed primitive is the taxonomy, and the taxonomy is human design work, not optimisation. The classifier comes later, when the rules have generated the data the classifier needs to be honest. Skipping a step does not save time; it changes the failure mode from *the rules disagreed and the founder rewrote them* to *the router collapsed and the founder couldn't see why*.

Episode 2 said the v0 is buildable today. The addendum agrees, with a sharper claim attached: it is buildable today *because* it is rules and instrumentation, not despite it. The rules are the product. The classifier is the version 2.
