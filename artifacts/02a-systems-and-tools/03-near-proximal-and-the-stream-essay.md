# Splitting the signal

The day before closed with a knowledge graph over my vault and my ops code — `borai-graph`, 3,916 nodes, 1,614,528 edges, a retrieval engine for every agent skill in my Claude Code setup to query before drafting anything. The retrospective I published at the close of that session named the residue honestly: 91.6% of those edges — 1,478,598 of them — were every-pair mtime-driven `follows`/`precedes` relationships, generated by within-directory cross-products at ingest. Four hundred and twenty-five edges per node, most of them temporal coincidence rather than signal. The ranker was drowning in them. The skill queries felt the confusion before anyone else did — a funding query surfacing a scene about typography, a content draft pulling the wrong receipt.

## The three options

The retrospective offered three defensible shapes for a fix.

Drop the temporal edges entirely. Clean, aggressive. Eliminates 91.6% of the graph in one stroke and forces all sequential reasoning through the narrower `authored_during` cross-file temporal window. Loses a signal that is cheap when it actually means something.

Cap the temporal edges at distance 1. Keep the closest-neighbour signal — the chunk that immediately precedes or follows this one in the same file — and drop the long-range every-pair bloat. The retrospective's recommendation.

Keep every-pair and downweight. Flatten the 1/d decay curve so distant siblings contribute less to the ranker without being excluded. Most conservative. Still leaves the ranker chewing through hundreds of per-node edges on every query.

The retrospective's call was cap-at-1. A sober recommendation. The least ambitious of the three.

## The fourth shape

I went somewhere else. Cap the *stored* edge at distance 1 — the retrospective's call for the stored shape — but compute distance > 1 at query time with a proper relevance signal. Two distinct mechanisms for two different jobs.

`near` — a stored edge at distance 1 within a file. Bidirectional. The closest prior chunk and the closest next chunk, always there for cheap traversal when the query needs *what's right next to this*. Legible in the schema. After the rebuild: roughly 1.9 near edges per node, down from 425.

`proximal` — a *computed* relation, surfaced only at query time, for siblings at distance > 1 within a file. Not stored. The signal is relevance-ranked, not distance-ranked: `α·(1/d) + (1−α)·cosine(seed_vec, target_vec)`. The embeddings are already paid for at ingest. Refusing to use them at retrieval time was the cheapness I did not want to inherit.

The design frame had shifted. The retrospective was thinking *cut the noise*. This was thinking *split the signal*. Become more specific, not less.

## The blend

The sage's pure-decay recommendation would have worked. It is the simpler call. What it leaves on the table is information I have already spent tokens on — one embedding per chunk, sitting in the graph, doing nothing at retrieval time. A retrieval engine that only uses distance is one waiting to be retrained on signal it already has.

The blend's real cost is not compute. It is tuning. α = 0.5 is the starting position; it is the name for a decision I do not yet have evidence to make well. The end-to-end query on *"temporal edge decision"* came back with proximal scores in the 0.35–0.37 range — a reasonable first number, not a proof. Retuning α per query type — higher α for structural traversal, lower α for semantic similarity — is a follow-on scene I can run against real skill queries once they accumulate.

Fallback to pure decay is a single environment variable flip. The experiment is reversible. The anxiety is not load-bearing; the decision is.

## The tag layer

The same PR introduced a second shape I had been putting off. Tags on the vault had always been whatever the author typed into the YAML frontmatter — sovereign, inconsistent, partial. A scene might carry `tags: [chapter-2a, borai-graph, retrieval]` and miss the `campaign:command-centre` that a query would want to scope on. Worse, the author tags cannot be relied on to follow a vocabulary — every writer invents their own.

The fix was two layers. Author tags keep their sovereignty — whatever YAML says, stays. Alongside them, the indexer mints a structured layer from the path and frontmatter: `type:scene`, `status:in-progress`, `chapter:02a-systems-and-tools`, `campaign:command-centre`. The two coexist via a reserved-prefix contract: any author tag that collides with a reserved prefix is stripped at ingest. The minted layer stays sovereign over its own vocabulary; the author layer stays sovereign over everything else.

Querying now routes cleanly. *Give me every concluded scene in chapter 2a* becomes a tag intersection over the minted layer. *Give me everything tagged borai-graph* still hits the author layer. No collision, no ambiguity, no guessing which writer used which word.

## Shallow by default

The third shape was the breaking change. The old `query()` returned full `RetrievalResult`s — every matching chunk's body included in the response. Good for a REPL. Terrible for a webapp whose first render needs to be a candidate list, not a token-budget fight over chunk bodies.

The new shape: `query(mode="shallow" | "full")`, defaulting to shallow. A shallow result carries the node id, the frontmatter, the tags, and a stable `expand()` handle. The body arrives only when the caller asks — `engine.expand(handle)` returns the full chunk post-hoc.

This inverts the posture. The old default said *here is everything*. The new default says *surface me the names first; I'll ask for the body when I want it*. The webapp's first render is now the list a user wants to scan, not the wall of text a token budget cannot afford. The shape of the retrieval API is the shape the webapp needs.

It is a breaking change for every caller. The four skill one-liners in `~/.claude/commands/` — `build-in-public-engine`, `funding-tracker`, `hackathon-radar`, `delegate-agent` — all hit `engine.query()` today. They will be updated in the next scene, probably alongside the delegate-agent integration work. The breaking change earns its place because the old default was a webapp tax I was going to pay forever.

## What landed

Reingest ran for eighteen minutes and forty seconds. Graph state at close: 3,968 nodes, 144,482 edges — an 89% reduction. Zero `follows`/`precedes`. 7,640 `near` edges, within-file adjacency only. 136,842 `relates_to` edges, content similarity, untouched by this scene.

End-to-end validation on *"temporal edge decision"* returned 53 results cleanly split by mechanism: `{seed: 5, near: 10, proximal: 14, relates_to: 24}`. Every layer firing, every result with clean provenance in its `reason` field. A separate query on *"Korea F-6 founder visa"* surfaced the founder profile and eligibility checker at 0.79 similarity — tag-scoped routing behaving as intended.

The test suite closed at 135 passing, up from 107. Four cheap riders bundled in the same PR: the Ollama `/api/embed` migration, deletion of the vestigial `/mnt/skills/` path rules in the source-type inference, periodic progress logs on ingest, and a `--if-present` guard on the BorAI pre-push hook's typecheck step.

## The pattern

The sage was not wrong. Cap-at-1 was a defensible call. What it lacked was ambition for the signal already bought and paid for.

The project's thesis is that specificity compounds. A retrieval engine that treats every within-file sibling as the same undifferentiated stream is an engine that cannot tell sibling-that-matters from sibling-that-happens-to-share-a-file. Splitting the signal is the thesis running at the infrastructure layer. The founder's tool becomes more sophisticated because the founder noticed the undifferentiated stream was noise, not because a better algorithm arrived.

Become more specific, not less. That was the frame that earned its place — over *cut the noise*, over *keep everything and weight it*, over *follow the sage's recommendation*. The graph now carries a shape the webapp can open onto.
