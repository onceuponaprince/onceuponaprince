# Design authority lives in the asset register

I spent half an hour running a research swarm to pick a domain for a study-buddy landing page. The orchestrator produced a careful recommendation. `studybuddy.page` over `studybuddy.xyz`, with reasoning about what `.page` signalled to a domain-checking founder, what the cost differential implied about commitment, what the keyword overlap with the existing product would do for cold landing visitors. Sound thinking. I read it, agreed with it, and proceeded to ignore it — because I already owned `teenyweeny.studio`.

The deciding input wasn't a recommendation. It was an asset.

This is the part of solo-build orchestration the orchestration literature does not quite catch. When you spin up a research swarm, the swarm produces deliberation. When you make the call, the call comes from a register the swarm cannot see — what you have already paid for, what you have already named, what your friends already know about. Design authority sits with the asset register, not with the analysis.

It is not that the analysis was wrong. The analysis was a different scene's analysis. The scene where the decision sat with cold logic — that scene did not run today. Today's scene was a quiet pivot from the editorial-scholarly register I had been writing into to the zine register a domain I already owned could carry. The pivot was load-bearing. URL-as-wordmark means no logo to commission, no separate brand name to defend, no two-word mark to source fonts for. The pivot reduced Chapter 2b's design surface — though the reduction was a side-effect of the asset-ownership input, not a deliberate design call.

Worth naming because the cost is real. The orchestrator drafted reasoning that never weighed in. The work did not lose. It simply stopped being the deciding input the moment an owned asset entered the room. Worth knowing because it shapes what you put in front of the swarm next time — what you ask the swarm to weigh, what you do not.

A second beat. The scene closed without a URL.

The declared deliverable was a live URL — the page that would let the Shareability signal fire, the page that would tell whether Chapter 2b's *landing before build* rule actually held. What shipped instead was specs. Two of them. The 2026-04-21 editorial-scholarly direction, then the 2026-04-22 zine direction superseding it. Both files left in place — the superseded-spec pattern, kept whole, becomes the first instance of a vault rule earning its keep.

But specs are not landing pages. The instrumentation remains unfireable. The scene produced a plan to ship; it did not ship.

The superseded-spec pattern earns its keep here for a specific reason. When the second spec replaces the first, you keep both files. The two together read as a decision rather than a silent overwrite. Delete the older spec and the pivot becomes invisible — the vault no longer carries a record of the moment the register changed. Keep both, and the pivot is legible as a moment of design authority moving from one register to another. The two files together *are* the decision.

That distinction matters when the decision was driven by an owned asset rather than a deliberation outcome. Without both specs in place, the move from `studybuddy.page` to `teenyweeny.studio` would read as either fickleness or as a quiet override. With both specs, it reads as what it was — the asset register entering the room and changing the deciding input. The pattern that names the move makes the move legible.

Two compounds emerge. One: when an owned asset enters the room, the deliberation that came before it does not lose, but it stops being the deciding input. Worth knowing because it shapes how to compose the deliberation in the first place — what you put in front of the swarm, what you keep in your asset register and out of the swarm's frame. Two: superseded specs left in place are how the vault tells the story of a pivot. Delete the old one and the pivot becomes invisible. Keep both and the pivot reads as a decision rather than a silent overwrite.

What this means for the chapter: Scene 2b-01b — *ship the URL* — opens next. Founder-led BorAI session. Register `teenyweeny.studio`, create the Supabase project, verify the Resend sending domain, link Vercel, scaffold the landing route per the 2026-04-22 spec. Five blockers, all external dependencies, all founder-only. Until those land, Scene 2b-02 (parser) cannot open. Chapter 2b's *landing before build* rule holds the line.

The chapter asks the harder question — *does the method produce a saleable product?* — and this scene cannot answer it yet. The narrative is running ahead of the product. That is the chapter's chosen pattern. The scene honours it but also inherits its cost. No URL means no signal, which means commercial proof sits one scene further out than the chapter arc implied on paper.

A scene that ships specs instead of a URL is not a failed scene. It is a scene that produced design authority in a legible form — two specs, one superseded, both filed — and a Conclude block that names where the deciding input came from. The next scene opens with that authority intact and a smaller, sharper deliverable: register the domain, scaffold the route, fire the signal.
