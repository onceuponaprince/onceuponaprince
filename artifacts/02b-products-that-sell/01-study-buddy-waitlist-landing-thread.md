# Thread — the orchestrator was right and I ignored it

1/ Spent half an hour running a research swarm to pick a domain for a study-buddy landing.

The swarm produced careful analysis. `studybuddy.page` over `.xyz`. Sound reasoning.

Then I picked `teenyweeny.studio` because I already owned it.

2/ The deciding input was not the analysis.

The deciding input was an asset already in my register.

That is where design authority sits in a solo build — not in the swarm's deliberation, in what you have already paid for and named.

3/ Worth naming because the cost is real.

The orchestrator drafted reasoning that never weighed in. The work did not lose. It just stopped being the deciding input the moment an owned asset entered the room.

Worth knowing because it shapes what you put in front of the swarm next time.

4/ Second beat: the scene shipped specs, not a URL.

The 2026-04-21 spec — editorial register.
The 2026-04-22 spec — zine register, superseding.

I left both in place.

5/ Superseded-spec pattern.

When the second spec replaces the first, you keep both files. The two together read as a decision rather than a silent overwrite.

Delete the old one and the pivot becomes invisible. Keep both and the pivot becomes legible.

This is the first instance of that pattern earning its keep in the vault.

6/ The hard part: a landing page without a URL is not a landing page.

The Shareability signal cannot fire on specs. The instrumentation needs the actual URL.

Scene 2b-01b opens next — register `teenyweeny.studio`, scaffold the landing, *then* the rest of Chapter 2b unlocks.

7/ Two compounds.

— When an owned asset enters the room, deliberation stops being the deciding input.

— Superseded specs left in place are how the vault tells the story of a pivot.

Both go on the named-pattern shelf.
